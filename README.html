When working with sinusoidal signals like the ones you can find in our current electricity distribution system, it is much easier to work with complex numbers like a+bj where, j^2 = -1. This is called the rectangular form of the complex number. In this way, numbers a and b mean the coordinates of the signal on the complex plane - the Real and Imaginary axis, respectively. In some cases it is advantageous to convert this form to the so called polar form where the important things are magnitude (size of the vector in the complex plane) and phase angle (the angle the vector holds with the Real axis). The representation of the signal in the polar form is called a phase vector or a phasor.

Your task is to create a class representing the phasor. It must be possible to instantiate this class using either rectangular or polar coordinates and convert between the two. Then you will have to implement the basic arithmetics of phasors - addition, subtraction, multiplication and division. This class could then be applied for example to an automatic solver of AC circuits.

So how to convert. The original AC signal has a general form A*cos(wt + theta) where A is the amplitude (peak), w is angular frequency in radians per second, t stands for time (the signal varies in time) and theta is the phase angle (how much the signal is shifted to the left or right from the normal cosine).

The phasor of such a signal preserves only the information about its amplitude, or magnitude, and phase so it's written as A‹theta. If you want to convert this into the rectangular form, the formula is a = A*cos(theta) - that is the coordinate on the real axis - and b = A*sin(theta) - which is the imaginary coordinate.

Now if you have the rectangular form and want to convert to the polar, the formulas are A = sqrt(a^2 + b^2) and theta = arctan(b/a). All these mathematical methods are shipped with C++ so you get them for free.

One important thing to keep in mind is that the angles that your program accepts and outputs must always be in degrees, contrary to what methods from the cmath library do. So you'll also have to know how to convert these angles to radians. Basically 360 degress is the same thing as 2pi radians. From that you can derive that to obtain radians from an angle in degrees, you have to multiply it by pi and then divide by 180. And if you want to go the other way, do the opposite, multiply the angle in radians by 180 and then divide by pi.

For more information, better explanations, graphs and all that you could wish to know, see Wikipedia.

Next you also need to know how to do basic arithmetic operations with the phasors and hopefully the conversions from above will start to make sense here. Adding and subtracting phasors is easier when you do it in the rectangular form and it works like this:

a1 + b1j + a2 + b2j = (a1 + a2) + (b1 + b2)j
a1 + b1j - (a2 + b2j) = (a1 - a2) + (b1 - b2)j

Multiplying and dividing phasors is on the contrary easier in the polar form and it works like this:

A1‹theta1 * A2‹theta2 = A1*A2‹(theta1 + theta2)
A1‹theta1 / A2‹theta2 = (A1/A2)‹(theta1 - theta2)

See? That's much easier than dividing complex numbers. So that's all that you need to know to complete the challenge. Good luck!
